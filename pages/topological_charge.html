About the topological charge

Summary
Topological charge is an observable that measures non-perturbative properties of the gauge field configuration. In even dimensions $d$, the topological charge is given by $$ Q = \alpha_{d}\int_\mathcal{M}d^{2n}x~\epsilon^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_d}~\text{Tr}~F_{\mu_1\mu_2}\cdots F_{\mu_{d-1}\mu_d} $$ where $\alpha_{2n}$ is some coefficient and $\mathcal{M}$ is the base manifold. It can be written more conveniently as a differential form as $$ Q=\alpha_{d}\int_{\mathcal{M}}\text{Tr}~F^{d/2}. $$ The topological charge is quantized. In 2 dimensions, only $U(1)$ gauge theory has a non-trivial topological structure, while in 4 dimensions, only $SU(N)$ has. Specifically, the topological charge for 2d $U(1)$ and 4d $SU(N)$ take the form $$ Q=\left\{ \begin{array}{l} \displaystyle \frac{1}{4\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}~\int d^2xF_{\mu\nu}&;\;\text{2d},\\ \displaystyle \frac{1}{32\pi^2}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}~\int d^4x\text{Tr}~F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}&;\;\text{4d}. \end{array} \right. $$ In the following sections, I will spend some time discussing why the topological charge only exists in some combination of dimensions and gauge groups. I will also determine the coefficient $\alpha_{d}$ at least for $d=2$ and $4$.
Chern-Simon form
First of all, it is known that the topological charge can be written as a surface integral of a Chern-Simon form $$ Q = \alpha_d\int_{\partial\mathcal{M}}\omega_{d-1}. $$ I don't know how to nicely prove this in arbitrary dimensions, but you can easily confirmed this for up to $d=6$: $$ \text{d}\omega_{d-1} &= \text{Tr}~F^{d/2};\\ \omega_1&=\text{Tr}A,\\ \omega_3&=\text{Tr}\left(A\text{d}A+\frac{2i}{3}A^3\right),\\ \omega_5&=\text{Tr}\left(A(\text{d}A)^2+\frac{3i}{2} A^3\text{d}A -\frac{3}{5}A^5\right), $$ where $F=dA+iA^2$. Note that some terms such as $\text{Tr}(A\text{d}A A\text{d}A)$ or $\text{Tr}A^2$ vanish because of the antisymmetricity of the exterior products. Typically, we are actually interested in either of the following: a closed base manifold or an infinitely extended base manifold with the condition that all fields vanish at the infinity. The second case can also be considered as a $d$-sphere $S^d$ with the infinity identified as one of the poles. We will now argue that the boundary $\partial\mathcal{M}$ for both cases can always be chosen to be homotopic to a $(d-1)$-sphere that can be shrunk to zero volume. Proof
The argument is rather simple. Instead of considering an integral over a closed manifold $\mathcal{M}$, we consider an integral over an open manifold $\mathcal{V}\subset\mathcal{M}$ such that $\mathcal{V}$ is $\mathcal{M}$ with a spherical puncture. As we take the limit where the puncture is shrunk to zero volume, we obtain the original integral over $\mathcal{M}$. Since the puncture is a $d$-ball, its boundary is a $(d-1)$-sphere. $Q.E.D.$ So the boundary $\partial\mathcal{M}$ actually should vanish in both cases. Naively, we therefore also expect $Q$ to also vanish. But actually, there is an exception to this when the integrand involves a multivalued function. Let's investigate this more carefully next.
Homotopy group
Consider an integral of a function $\omega(x)$ around a puncture $\partial\mathcal{M}=\gamma$ $$ I_\gamma = \oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x) $$ We now assume that $I_\gamma$ is multivalued with period $\Lambda$: $$ I_\gamma = \text{pv}\left[\oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x)\right]+q\Lambda;\quad q\in\mathbb{Z}, $$ where $\text{pv}[~\cdot~]$ is the principal value. Shrinking the puncture to zero makes the principal value vanish, leaving only the quantized term $q\Lambda$ $$ I_0 = q\Lambda. $$ What kind of $\omega(x)$ gives the integral with this property? The simplest example is when $\omega(x)$ is a total derivative of a multivalued function with period $\Lambda$: $$ \omega(x) = \partial_x f(x) $$ If we take $\gamma$ to be a circle $x\in[-\pi,\pi)$ and $f(x)$ to be periodic on $\gamma$ modulo $\Lambda$; $f(x+2\pi)\equiv f(x)~(\text{mod}~\Lambda)$, then we have $$ I_\gamma=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}dx \partial_xf(x)=f(\pi)-f(-\pi)=q\Lambda. $$ Here, the integer $q$ counts the number of times $f(x)$ crosses the branch cuts in the interval $x\in[-\pi,\pi)$. Another way to imagine this is to consider $f(x)$ as a map from $\gamma$ (a circle with radius $1$) onto another circle (with radius $\Lambda/2\pi$). Then $q$ counts the number of times the first circle wraps around the second circle. Let us try to generalize this statement. Let $\int\omega$ be a map from a space-time manifold $\partial\mathcal{M}$ onto the group manifold $\mathcal{G}$, then the existence of nonzero topological charge depends on wheter or not we can wrap $\partial\mathcal{M}$ around $\mathcal{G}$ in a non-trivial way. This problem can be catagorized by an object called the homotopy group $[\partial\mathcal{M},\mathcal{G}]$. In the previous example, it is known that the homotopy group of a map between two 1-spheres is $[S^1,S^1]=\mathbb{Z}$. This means that all possible distinct ways to wrap the circles can be parametrized by an integer $q$. For a map from $S^m$ to $S^n$, the homotopy group is typically denoted by $\pi_m(S^n):=[S^m,S^n]$. Before we summarize, let us state again that, in $d$-dimensions, we consider $\partial\mathcal{M}\simeq S^{d-1}$. We also note that $U(1)\simeq S^1$ and $SU(2)\simeq S^3$. Mapping onto other non-Abelian groups can be shown to be homotopic to a mapping onto one of their $SU(2)$ subgroups. This is a result from Bott periodicity theorem.
