In this section, we will derive some known properties of the topological charge in even dimensions. At the beginning, I will use the differential form for notational simplicity, then I will switch to the usual coefficient notation when we discuss specific cases.
Quantization
In $d=2n$ dimensions, the topological charge is given by $$ Q = \alpha_{2n}\int_\mathcal{M} \text{Tr}F^n. $$ Here, $F=\text{d}A+iA\wedge A$ is the curvature form, $F^{n}$ means the $n$-th power with respect to the exterior product, $\int_\mathcal{M}$ is the volume integral over the manifold $\mathcal{M}$, and $\alpha_{2n}$ is some coefficient that we will fix later. The integrand can be expressed as a sum of $2n$-forms $$ \text{Tr}F^n=\sum_{k=1}^{n} f_k\text{Tr}\{A^{2k-2}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k+1}\} $$ for some coefficients $f_k$ which I don't know the general form but it can be easily worked out by hand. Also, remember that $\text{Tr}A^{2n}=0$ because the cyclic property of the trace is incompatible with the even exterior power, so there is no $k=n+1$ in the sum. Note that $$ A^{2k-2}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k+1}=\frac{1}{2k-1}\text{d}(A^{2k-1}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k}). $$ It follows that the topological charge is an integral of a total derivative \begin{align} Q &= \alpha_{2n}\int_{\mathcal{M}} \text{d}\omega_{2n-1};\\ \omega_{2n-1}&=\sum_{k=1}^n\frac{f_k}{2k-1}\text{Tr}\{A^{2k-1}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k}\}. \end{align} The object $\omega_p$ is known as the Chern-Simon $p$-form. The topological charge is now replaced by the surface integral $$ Q = \alpha_{2n}\int_{\partial\mathcal{M}} \omega_{2n-1}. $$ We will now restrict ourselves to one of these two cases:
  • Compact manifold
  • Infinite manifold with all fields vanishing at the infinity
Open manifolds have totally different topological physics, so we will leave the discussions for other sections. The second case can be considered as a sphere with the infinity identified as one of the poles; $\mathbb{R}^d\cup\{\infty\}\simeq S^d$. In any case, we will now argue that the boundary $\partial\mathcal{M}$ for both cases can always be chosen to be homotopic to a $(d-1)$-sphere that can be shrunk to zero volume. The argument is rather simple. Instead of considering an integral over a closed manifold $\mathcal{M}$, we consider an integral over an open manifold $\mathcal{V}\subset\mathcal{M}$ such that $\mathcal{V}$ is $\mathcal{M}$ with a spherical puncture. As we take the limit where the puncture is shrunk to zero volume, we obtain the original integral over $\mathcal{M}$. Since the puncture is a $d$-ball, its boundary is a $(d-1)$-sphere. $Q.E.D.$ The remaining question now is: what happens to the surface integral over a shrinking $(d-1)$-sphere? Naively, you would think that the integral becomes zero. But as we will see, this may not be true when $\int\omega_{2n-1}$ is a multivalued function.
Homotopy groups
Consider an integral of a function $\omega(x)$ around a puncture $\partial\mathcal{M}=\gamma$ $$ I_\gamma = \oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x) $$ We now assume that $I_\gamma$ is multivalued with period $\Lambda$: $$ I_\gamma = \text{pv}\left[\oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x)\right]+q\Lambda;\quad q\in\mathbb{Z}, $$ where $\text{pv}[~\cdot~]$ is the principal value. Shrinking the puncture to zero makes the principal value vanish, leaving only the quantized term $q\Lambda$ $$ I_0 = q\Lambda. $$ What kind of $\omega(x)$ gives the integral with this property? The simplest example is when $\omega(x)$ is a total derivative of a multivalued function with period $\Lambda$: $$ \omega(x) = \partial_x f(x) $$ If we take $\gamma$ to be a circle $x\in[0,2\pi)$ and $f(x)$ to be periodic on $\gamma$ modulo $\Lambda$; $f(x+2\pi)\equiv f(x)~(\text{mod}~\Lambda)$, then we have $$ I_\gamma=\int_0^{2\pi}dx \partial_xf(x)=f(2\pi)-f(0)=q\Lambda. $$ Here, the integer $q$ counts the number of times $f(x)$ crosses the branch cuts in the interval $x\in[0,2\pi)$. Another way to imagine this is to consider $f(x)$ as a map from $\gamma$ (a circle with radius $1$) onto another circle (with radius $\Lambda/2\pi$). Then $q$ counts the number of times the first circle wraps around the second circle. Let us try to generalize this statement. Let $\int\omega$ be a map from a space-time manifold $\partial\mathcal{M}$ onto the group manifold $\mathcal{G}$, then the existence of nonzero topological charge depends on wheter or not we can wrap $\partial\mathcal{M}$ around $\mathcal{G}$ in a non-trivial way. This problem can be catagorized by an object called the homotopy group $[\partial\mathcal{M},\mathcal{G}]$. In the previous example, it is known that the homotopy group of a map between two 1-spheres is $[S^1,S^1]=\mathbb{Z}$. This means that all possible distinct ways to wrap the circles can be parametrized by an integer $q$. For a map from $S^m$ to $S^n$, the homotopy group is typically denoted by $\pi_m(S^n):=[S^m,S^n]$. Before we summarize, let us state again that, in $d$-dimensions, we consider $\partial\mathcal{M}\simeq S^{d-1}$. We also note that $U(1)\simeq S^1$ and $SU(2)\simeq S^3$. Mapping onto other non-Abelian groups can be shown to be homotopic to a mapping onto one of their $SU(2)$ subgroups. This is a result from Bott periodicity theorem. Here is a summarized table of important homotopy groups and their implications:

Pure gauge
So far, we have shown that the topological charge must be a multiple of integer if the base manifold and the gauge group are compatible. But we still have to determine the coefficient $\alpha_d$ that makes $Q\in\mathbb{Z}$. For simplicity of the calculation, we will consider the so called 'pure' gauge $$ A_\mu = -ig^{-1}\partial_\mu g. $$ Namely, it is the gauge transformation of $A_\mu=0$ with $V=g^{-1}$. See here if you forgot. Since $F=\text{d}A+iA\wedge A$ is gauge invariant, it follows that $F=0$. Or you can also show explicitly: \begin{align} F &= \text{d}A + iA\wedge A\\ &= \text{d}(-ig^{-1}\text{d}g)+i(-ig^{-1}\text{d}g)\wedge(-ig^{-1}\text{d}g)\\ &= -i\text{d}g^{-1}\wedge \text{d}g-i(g^{-1}\text{d}g)\wedge(g^{-1}\text{d}g)\\ &= -i(-g^{-1}\text{d}gg^{-1})\wedge \text{d}g-i(g^{-1}\text{d}g)\wedge(g^{-1}\text{d}g)=0, \end{align} where we have used $\text{d}g^{-1}=-g^{-1}\text{d}gg^{-1}$ (which comes from $\text{d}(gg^{-1})=0$). Physically, the fact that the field strength, and thus the kinetic action, vanish means that we are considering the classical solution to the Yang-Mills equation. As we discussed above, the topological charge is quantized. This means that even if we introduce a small quantum fluctuation, the topological charge should remains the same. The fact that $F=0$ also means that $$ A\wedge A = i\text{d}A. $$ This identity is weird, since it implies that $$ A^{p+1} = \frac{i^p}{p!}\text{d}^pA. $$ You can prove this starting from $A^2=i\text{d}A$, then moving to $A^3=iA\text{d}A=\frac{i}{2}\text{d}A^2=\frac{i^2}{2}\text{d}^2A$, etc. Another thing is, you can also easily show that $$ \omega_{d-1}=\beta_{d-1} \text{Tr}~A^{d-1} $$ for some coefficient $\beta_{d-1}$ which I don't knowthe general form but can be computed by hand. Consequently, you have $$ Q = \alpha_d\beta_{d-1}\frac{i^{d-2}}{(d-2)!}\times\int_{S^{d-1}}\text{d}^{d-2}\text{Tr}A. $$ We will now use the same argument as before: the integral of an exact form $\text{d}f$ over $S^p$ can be rewritten as an integration of $f$ over a shrinking $S^{p-1}$. Keep repeating this, and we finaly will arrive at an integral of $A$ over a shrinking $S^1$. Each layer of the transition $S^{p}\rightarrow S^{p-1}$, we gain some geometric factor. We will use this fact to find the coefficient $\alpha_d$.
Determining the coefficients
For now, let's focus on the integral $$ I_d:=\int_{S^{d-1}}\text{d}^{d-2}\text{Tr}A. $$ We can use the hyper-spherical coordinates to compute the integral \begin{align} I_d &= \int_0^\pi d\phi (\sin\phi)^{d-2} I_{d-1};\quad\text{for}~d>2,\\ I_2 &= \int_{-\pi}^\pi d\theta \text{Tr}A_\theta =-i\int_{-\pi}^\pi d\theta\partial_\theta\text{Tr}\log g(\theta) =-i\text{Tr}\log(g(\pi)g^{-1}(-\pi)). \end{align} In the last line, we use the fact that $A_\theta=-ig^{-1}\partial_\theta g=-i\partial_\theta\log g$. For $d=2n$, you can integrate two layers at a time and obtain $$ I_d=\frac{\pi}{(d/2)}I_{d-2}=\frac{\pi^{(d/2)-1}}{(d/2)!}I_2=-i\frac{\pi^{(d/2)-1}}{(d/2)!}\text{Tr}\log(g(\pi)g^{-1}(-\pi)). $$ We will now consider a gauge transformation $g$ that is periodic in $\theta$ but wrapped around the gauge manifold $q$ times: $$ g(\theta) = \exp(iq\theta\sigma) $$ where $\sigma=1$ for U(1) and $\sigma=t_1$ (the first generator) for $SU(N)$.
