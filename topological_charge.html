About topological charge

In this section, we will derive some known properties of the topological charge in even dimensions. At the beginning, I will use the differential form for notational simplicity, then I will switch to the usual coefficient notation when we discuss specific cases.
Quantization
In $d=2n$ dimensions, the topological charge is given by $$ Q = \alpha_{2n}\int_\mathcal{M} \text{Tr}F^n. $$ Here, $F=\text{d}A+iA\wedge A$ is the curvature form, $F^{n}$ means the $n$-th power with respect to the exterior product, $\int_\mathcal{M}$ is the volume integral over the manifold $\mathcal{M}$, and $\alpha_{2n}$ is some coefficient that we will fix later. The integrand can be expressed as a sum of $2n$-forms $$ \text{Tr}F^n=\sum_{k=1}^{n} f_k\text{Tr}\{A^{2k-2}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k+1}\} $$ for some coefficients $f_k$. Also, remember that $\text{Tr}A^{2n}=0$ because the cyclic property of the trace is incompatible with the even exterior power, so there is no $k=n+1$ in the sum. Note that $$ A^{2k-2}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k+1}=\frac{1}{2k-1}\text{d}(A^{2k-1}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k}). $$ It follows that the topological charge is an integral of a total derivative \begin{align} Q &= \alpha_{2n}\int_{\mathcal{M}} \text{d}\omega_{2n-1};\\ \omega_{2n-1}&=\sum_{k=1}^n\frac{f_k}{2k-1}\text{Tr}\{A^{2k-1}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k}\}. \end{align} The object $\omega_p$ is known as the Chern-Simon $p$-form. The topological charge is now replaced by the surface integral $$ Q = \alpha_{2n}\int_{\partial\mathcal{M}} \omega_{2n-1}. $$ We will now restrict ourselves to one of these two cases:
  • Compact manifold
  • Infinite manifold with all fields vanishing at the infinity
Open manifolds have totally different topological physics, so we will leave the discussions for other sections. The second case can be considered as a sphere with the infinity identified as one of the poles; $\mathbb{R}^d\cup\{\infty\}\simeq S^d$. In any case, we will now argue that the boundary $\partial\mathcal{M}$ for both cases can always be chosen to be homotopic to a $(d-1)$-sphere that can be shrunk to zero volume. The argument is rather simple. Instead of considering an integral over a closed manifold $\mathcal{M}$, we consider an integral over an open manifold $\mathcal{V}\subset\mathcal{M}$ such that $\mathcal{V}$ is $\mathcal{M}$ with a spherical puncture. As we take the limit where the puncture is shrunk to zero volume, we obtain the original integral over $\mathcal{M}$. Since the puncture is a $d$-ball, its boundary is a $(d-1)$-sphere. $Q.E.D.$ The remaining question now is: what happens to the surface integral over a shrinking $(d-1)$-sphere? Naively, you would think that the integral becomes zero. But as we will see, this may not be true when $\int\omega_{2n-1}$ is a multivalued function.
Homotopy groups
Consider an integral of a function $\omega(x)$ around a puncture $\partial\mathcal{M}=\gamma$ $$ I_\gamma = \oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x) $$ We now assume that $I_\gamma$ is multivalued with period $\Lambda$: $$ I_\gamma = \text{pv}\left[\oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x)\right]+q\Lambda;\quad q\in\mathbb{Z}, $$ where $\text{pv}[~\cdot~]$ is the principal value. Shrinking the puncture to zero makes the principal value vanish, leaving only the quantized term $q\Lambda$ $$ I_0 = q\Lambda. $$ What kind of $\omega(x)$ gives the integral with this property? The simplest example is when $\omega(x)$ is a total derivative of a multivalued function with period $\Lambda$: $$ \omega(x) = \partial_x f(x) $$ If we take $\gamma$ to be a circle $x\in[0,2\pi)$ and $f(x)$ to be periodic on $\gamma$ modulo $\Lambda$; $f(x+2\pi)\equiv f(x)~(\text{mod}~\Lambda)$, then we have $$ I_\gamma=\int_0^{2\pi}dx \partial_xf(x)=f(2\pi)-f(0)=q\Lambda. $$ Here, the integer $q$ counts the number of times $f(x)$ crosses the branch cuts in the interval $x\in[0,2\pi)$. Another way to imagine this is to consider $f(x)$ as a map from $\gamma$ (a circle with radius $1$) onto another circle (with radius $\Lambda/2\pi$). Then $q$ counts the number of times the first circle wraps around the second circle. Let us try to generalize this statement. Let $\int\omega$ be a map from a space-time manifold $\partial\mathcal{M}$ onto the group manifold $\mathcal{G}$, then the existence of nonzero topological charge depends on wheter or not we can wrap $\partial\mathcal{M}$ around $\mathcal{G}$ in a non-trivial way. This problem can be catagorized by an object called the homotopy group $[\partial\mathcal{M},\mathcal{G}]$. In the previous example, it is known that the homotopy group of a map between two 1-spheres is $[S^1,S^1]=\mathbb{Z}$. This means that all possible distinct ways to wrap the circles can be parametrized by an integer $q$. For a map from $S^m$ to $S^n$, the homotopy group is typically denoted by $\pi_m(S^n):=[S^m,S^n]$. Before we summarize, let us state again that, in $d$-dimensions, we consider $\partial\mathcal{M}\simeq S^{d-1}$. We also note that $U(1)\simeq S^1$ and $SU(2)\simeq S^3$. Mapping onto other non-Abelian groups can be shown to be homotopic to a mapping onto one of their $SU(2)$ subgroups. This is from Botts theorem. Here is a summarized table of important homotopy groups and their implications:
Dimension $d$ Gauge group Homotopy group Topological structure
$2$ $U(1)$ $\pi_1(U(1))=\mathbb{Z}$ $\mathbb{Z}$
$2$ $SU(N)$ $\pi_1(SU(N))\sim\pi_1(SU(2))=0$ trivial
$d>2$ $U(1)$ $\pi_{d-1}(U(1))=0$ trivial
$4$ $SU(N)$ $\pi_3(SU(N))\sim\pi_3(SU(2))=\mathbb{Z}$ $\mathbb{Z}$

See here for other homotopy groups. We will now turn our attention to the two non-trivial cases on the table: 2d $U(1)$ and 4d $SU(N)$ gauge theories.
2d $U(1)$
The $U(1)$ topological charge in 2 dimensions reads \begin{align} Q&=\alpha_2\int_\mathcal{M}d^2x~\epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu} &(\text{volume form})\\ &=\alpha_2 \lim_{r\rightarrow0}r\int_{-\pi}^\pi d\theta~\epsilon^{\mu\nu}n_\mu A_\nu &(\text{surface form}) \end{align} where $n_\mu$ is the normal vector perpendicular to the surface $\partial\mathcal{M}\simeq S^1$. The measure $\int_{-\pi}^\pi d\theta$ is the surface integration measure for $S^1$. And finally, the limit $r\rightarrow0$ corresponds to taking the zero puncture limit. We will consider the gauge field around the puncture to take the form $$ A_\mu = A^\text{avr}_\mu - ig^{-1}\partial_\mu g $$ where $A^\text{avr}_\mu$ is the average part of the gauge field along $\partial\mathcal{M}$, which will cancel out in the integral. As for the gauge transformation part, we need $g\in U(1)$ to be periodic along $\partial\mathcal{M}$, so we consider $$g(r,\theta) = \exp(i q\theta ),$$ giving \begin{align} Q &= i\alpha_2 \lim_{r\rightarrow0}r\int_{-\pi}^\pi d\theta~\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log g\\ &= \alpha_2 \lim_{r\rightarrow0}\int_{-\pi}^\pi d\theta~q\\ &=2\pi q\alpha_2. \end{align} From this result, we set $\alpha_2=1/{2\pi}$ so that $Q$ is integer-valued. Summary for 2d $U(1)$: \begin{align} Q&=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_\mathcal{M} d^2x \epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu},\\ \omega^\mu_1&=\epsilon^{\mu\nu}A_\nu \end{align}
4d $SU(2)$
The $SU(2)$ topological charge in 4 dimensions reads \begin{align} Q&=\alpha_4\int_\mathcal{M}d^4x~\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\text{Tr}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma} &(\text{volume form})\\ &=\alpha_4 \lim_{r\rightarrow0}r\int_{S^3} d\Omega_3~\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} n_\mu \text{Tr}\left(A_\nu \partial_\rho A_\sigma+\frac{2i}{3} A_\nu A_\rho A_\sigma\right). &(\text{surface form}) \end{align} We will consider the pure gauge $A_\mu = -ig^{-1}\partial_\mu g$ for simplicity. It this gauge, the field strength vanishes \begin{align} F &= \text{d}A + iA\wedge A\\ &= \text{d}(-ig^{-1}\text{d}g)+i(-ig^{-1}\text{d}g)\wedge(-ig^{-1}\text{d}g)\\ &= -i\text{d}g^{-1}\wedge \text{d}g-i(g^{-1}\text{d}g)\wedge(g^{-1}\text{d}g)\\ &= -i(-g^{-1}\text{d}gg^{-1})\wedge \text{d}g-i(g^{-1}\text{d}g)\wedge(g^{-1}\text{d}g)=0, \end{align} where we have used the fact that $\text{d}g^{-1}=-g^{-1}\text{d}gg^{-1}$ (which comes from $\text{d}(gg^{-1})=0$). One consequence of this is that we are considering the local minima of the kinetic action $\int F\wedge\star F$, a.k.a. the classical solutions. Adding small $A^\text{avr}$ to the gauge field will slightly increase the action, but will not change the topological charge since, from our earlier discussion, the charge is quantized. In any case, since $F=0$ in this case, we have a nice relation $$ dA = -iA\wedge A\quad\text{(pure gauge).} $$ Plugging this into the Chern-Simon term gives \begin{align} \omega^\mu_3 &= -\frac{i}{3}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} \text{Tr}\left(A_\nu A_\rho A_\sigma\right)\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} \text{Tr}\left( g^{-1}\partial_\nu g g^{-1}\partial_\rho g g^{-1}\partial_\sigma g \right) \end{align} Since we will take $n_\mu$ to be orthonormal to the surface $\partial\mathcal{M}=S^3$, we can simply consider only the 3d components of the Chern-Simon form: $$ \omega_3 = \frac{1}{3}\epsilon^{ijk} \text{Tr}\left( g^{-1}\partial_i g g^{-1}\partial_j g g^{-1}\partial_k g \right) $$ so that $$ Q = \alpha_4 \int_{S^3} d\Omega_3~\omega_3. $$ (now what...)
