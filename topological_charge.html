About topological charge

In this section, we will derive some known properties of the topological charge in even dimensions. At the beginning, I will use the differential form for notational simplicity, then I will switch to the usual coefficient notation when we discuss specific cases.
Quantization
In $d=2n$ dimensions, the topological charge is given by $$ Q = \alpha_{2n}\int_\mathcal{M} \text{Tr}F^n. $$ Here, $F=\text{d}A+iA\wedge A$ is the curvature form, $F^{n}$ means the $n$-th power with respect to the exterior product, $\int_\mathcal{M}$ is the volume integral over the manifold $\mathcal{M}$, and $\alpha_{2n}$ is some coefficient that we will fix later. The integrand can be expressed as a sum of $2n$-forms $$ \text{Tr}F^n=\sum_{k=1}^{n} f_k\text{Tr}\{A^{2k-2}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k+1}\} $$ for some coefficients $f_k$. Also, remember that $\text{Tr}A^{2n}=0$ because the cyclic property of the trace is incompatible with the even exterior power, so there is no $k=n+1$ in the sum. Note that $$ A^{2k-2}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k+1}=\frac{1}{2k-1}\text{d}(A^{2k-1}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k}). $$ It follows that the topological charge is an integral of a total derivative \begin{align} Q &= \alpha_{2n}\int_{\mathcal{M}} \text{d}\omega_{2n-1};\\ \omega_{2n-1}&=\sum_{k=1}^n\frac{f_k}{2k-1}\text{Tr}\{A^{2k-1}\wedge (\text{d}A)^{n-k}\}. \end{align} The object $\omega_p$ is known as the Chern-Simon $p$-form.

The topological charge is now replaced by the surface integral $$ Q = \alpha_{2n}\int_{\partial\mathcal{M}} \omega_{2n-1}. $$ We will now restrict ourselves to one of these two cases:
  • Compact manifold
  • Infinite manifold with all fields vanishing at the infinity
Open manifolds have totally different topological physics, so we will leave the discussions for other sections. The second case can be considered as a sphere with the infinity identified as one of the poles; $\mathbb{R}^d\cup\{\infty\}\simeq S^d$. In any case, we will now argue that the boundary $\partial\mathcal{M}$ for both cases can always be chosen to be homotopic to a $(d-1)$-sphere that can be shrunk to zero volume.

The argument is rather simple. Instead of considering an integral over a closed manifold $\mathcal{M}$, we consider an integral over an open manifold $\mathcal{V}\subset\mathcal{M}$ such that $\mathcal{V}$ is $\mathcal{M}$ with a spherical puncture. As we take the limit where the puncture is shrunk to zero volume, we obtain the original integral over $\mathcal{M}$. Since the puncture is a $d$-ball, its boundary is a $(d-1)$-sphere . $Q.E.D.$

The remaining question now is: what happens to the surface integral over a shrinking $(d-1)$-sphere? Naively, you would think that the integral becomes zero. But as we will see, this may not be true when $\omega_{2n-1}$ is a multivalued function.
Homotopy groups
Consider an integral of a multivalued function $\omega(x)$ around a puncture $\gamma$ $$ I_\gamma = \oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x) $$ Assuming that $\omega(x)$ is multivalued with period $\Lambda$: $$\omega(x) = \text{pv}[\omega(x)] + \Lambda\times\text{integer}$$ where $\text{pv}[~\cdot~]$ is the principal value. Then the surface integral around the puncture is also multivalued: $$ I_\gamma = \text{pv}\left[\oint_\gamma dx~\omega(x)\right]+q\Lambda;\quad q\in\mathbb{Z}. $$ Shrinking the puncture to zero makes the principal value vanish, leaving only the quantized term $q\Lambda$ $$ I_0 = q\Lambda. $$ Here comes the catch: we still need to prove that the remainder terms do not cancel out and leave $q=0$ at the end. For $\gamma$ being a circle and $\omega$ being a real function modulo $\Lambda$, this is rather easy to confirm. For example, if we consider $\gamma:x\in[0,a)$ and $\omega(x)=q\Lambda x/a$, then we have $$ I_\gamma=\int_0^adx \frac{q\Lambda x}{a}= q\Lambda,$$ which remains to be this value even after taking $a\rightarrow0$. In this example, $\omega(x)$ crosses the branch cut $q$ times: at $x=\frac{a}{q},~2\frac{a}{q},~\cdots,~(q-1)\frac{a}{q},~a$. Another way to look at this example is to consider $\omega$ to be a map from one circle (with radius $a/2\pi$) to another circle (with radius $\Lambda/2\pi$). Then $q$ counts the number of times the first circle is wrapped around the other circle.

Let us try to generalize this statement. Let $\omega$ be a map from a space-time manifold $\partial\mathcal{M}$ onto the group manifold $\mathcal{G}$, then the existence of nonzero topological charge depends on wheter or not we can wrap $\partial\mathcal{M}$ around $\mathcal{G}$ in a non-trivial way. This problem can be catagorized by an object called the homotopy group $[\partial\mathcal{M},\mathcal{G}]$ . In the previous example, it is known that the homotopy group of a map between two 1-spheres is $[S^1,S^1]=\mathbb{Z}$. This means that all possible distinct ways to wrap the circles can be parametrized by an integer $q$. For a map from $S^m$ to $S^n$, the homotopy group is typically denoted by $\pi_m(S^n):=[S^m,S^n]$.

Before we summarize, let us state again that, in $d$-dimensions, we consider $\partial\mathcal{M}\simeq S^{d-1}$. We also note that $U(1)\simeq S^1$ and $SU(2)\simeq S^3$. Mapping onto other non-Abelian groups can be shown to be homotopic to a mapping onto one of their $SU(2)$ subgroups. This is from Botts theorem .
